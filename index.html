The Trombone Journey

Learning JavaScript felt like standing in front of an instrument case I'd carried around for years and never opened.

Calling things by name

The Slide

A trombone slide is like a blank canvas, one where the pitch is defined by the position. A small change can mean a big difference. Variables felt like that. let and const were my first tools for saying "this thing exists, and it's called this." I was naming what I wanted to be true instead of just describing whats on the page. That distinction between standard and defined changed everything about how I thought about writing code.

Reading the instructions

Sheet Music

Sheet music is a set of conditions. If the note sits on the third line of the staff, you play B. That's it — no ambiguity, no interpretation, just a rule that fires when the situation matches. Conditionals worked the same way. If this, do that. And with classList, suddenly a website could look at itself and decide to change. Not on page load, not because I hard-coded a state. Because a condition was true. That was the moment websites stopped feeling like documents to me and started feeling like something alive.

Waiting to be played

The Mouthpiece

The mouthpiece is where the player meets the instrument. It listens for what you give it, whether it's a soft breath or a sharp attack, and converts that input into something the rest of the horn can work with. addEventListener was my mouthpiece. I could write a function and wait for a click, a keypress, or a scroll. The user could arrive and change what the page did. A website with a function, not just a form. Something that responded instead of just being there.

One source of truth

The Tuning Slide

If every player in an orchestra had its own idea of what "in tune" meant, you'd have noise, not music. The tuning slide is the single adjustment that governs the whole instrument's pitch. CSS custom properties hit me the same way. Set a color, a spacing value, a type size — set it once, in one place — and everything that references it moves together. I'd built sites before where changing a button color meant hunting through four files. Design tokens fixed that by setting it from the start.

The whole instrument

The Trombone

No single part of a trombone makes music on its own. The slide without the bell is a metal tube. The mouthpiece without the slide is a useless cup. It's only when everything works together that you get music. I'm not going to say I've mastered JavaScript. Animation still gives me trouble, and there are concepts I circle back to and have to relearn. But I understand design systems now in a way I didn't before. I started treating code like an instrument I was assembling piece by piece. The case is open and music is being played.
